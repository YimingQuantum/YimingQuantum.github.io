1. Algorithm

Shor‚Äôs algorithm is the first quantum algorithm that authentically raised quantum computing to a fairly high position because it can factor an odd composite positive integer in polynomial time, which threatens the RSA encryption fundamentally.

It can be proved that the factoring problem can reduced to the order-finding problem. We first introduce the quantum algorithm of order-finding, then explain how to realize that reduction. Furthermore, as we‚Äôll see later, the quantum order-finding algorithm is just an application of QPE.

For those who are not familiar with QPE, click here.

2.1) Order-finding

The Order-finding problem is that for some given \( x, N\in \mathbb{N^+}\), find the least positive integer \( r\), s.t.
$$
x^r = 1\ (\text{mod}\ N)
$$
where \(r\) is called the order of \(x\) modulo \(N\). It can be proved that \( r<N\).

Here we introduce the operator \( U\) (oracle) to solve the order-finding problem, which satisfies
$$
U |y\rangle\equiv |xy\ (\text{mod}\ N)\rangle
$$
for \( y\in[0,N)\) and \( x,y\in\mathbb{N^+}\). Because the input \(y \) we‚Äôll discuss later satisfies \( y<N\), therefore for \( y\ge N\), we require \( U|y\rangle=|y\rangle\), which is a trivial operation. Remember again that these are just definitions and the implementation of \( U\) has already been given.

For
$$
|u_s\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi isk/r} |x^k\ \text{mod }N\rangle
$$
it can be checked that \( \{|u_s\rangle | s\in\mathbb{N^{+}},\ s\in[0,r)\} \) are the eigenstates of \( U\) since
$$
\begin{split}
U|u_s\rangle = &\frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi isk/r} |x^{k+1}\ \text{mod }N\rangle \\
=&\frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{p=1}^{r}e^{-2\pi is(p-1)/r} |x^p\ \text{mod }N\rangle\\
=&e^{2\pi is/r}\frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{p=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi isp/r} |x^p\ \text{mod }N\rangle \\
=&e^{2\pi is/r}|u_s\rangle
\end{split}
$$
where \( p=k+1\).

Our goal is to achieve the value of \( r\), therefore by applying QPE to \( U\) and \( |u_s\rangle\), we can estimate \( r\) for any given accuracy \(t\). Now the problem turns to preparation of \( |u_s\rangle\). Actually, for a specified \(s\), we cannot prepare \( |u_s\rangle\) because that assumes we‚Äôve already known \( r\) in advance.

Notice that
$$
\begin{split}
&\frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{s=0}^{r-1}|u_s\rangle \\
= &\frac{1}{r}\sum_{s=0}^{r-1}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi isk/r}|x^k\ \text{mod }N\rangle \\
= &\frac{1}{r}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}\bigg (
\sum_{s=0}^{r-1}e^{-2\pi isk/r}\bigg )|x^k\ \text{mod }N\rangle \\
=&\frac{1}{r}\sum_{k=0}^{r-1}\delta_{k0}|x^k\ \text{mod }N\rangle \\
=& |1\rangle
\end{split}
$$
hence rather than prepare \( |u_s\rangle\), by preparing the state \( |1\rangle\) in the second register of QPE, we‚Äôre able to construct a superposition of all the eigenstates \( \{|u_s\rangle\}\) .

$$
\varphi_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^t}} \sum_{j=0}^{2^t-1}|j\rangle |x^j\ \text{mod }N\rangle
$$
After implementing the inverse QFT to [‚ñ≤], we get
$$
\varphi_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}\sum_{s=0}^{r-1}|\tilde{s/r}\rangle|u_s\rangle
$$
In the end, we measure the first register to get the estimation of \( s/r\), which is rational number.

If \( s\) and \(r \) have common factors, this estimation will fail, while it can be proved that
$$
\text{prob}\bigg (\text{gcd}(s,r)=1\bigg )\ge \frac{1}{2\log(r)} > \frac{1}{2\log(N)}
$$
where gcd means greatest common divisor. Therefore we can achieve \( s/r\) where \( s\) and \(r\) are coprime by repeating the algorithm at most \( 2\log (N)\) times on average.

2.2) Reduction

Suppose

  • \( N\) is an \(L\)-bit odd composite positive integer, whose prime factorization is
    $$
    N = p_1^{\alpha_1}p_2^{\alpha_2}\cdots p_m^{\alpha_m}
    $$

  • \( x \in[1,N)\) is a randomly chosen integer co-prime to \(N\).

  • \(r\) is the order of \(x\) modulo \(N\).

then

  • (Theorem 1)
    $$
    \text{prob}\bigg (r\text{ is even and }x^{r/2}\neq -1\ (\text{mod }N)\bigg )\ge 1-\frac{1}{2^m}
    $$
  • (Theorem 2)
    If \( y\) is a non-trivial solution to the equation
    $$
    y^2 = 1(\text{ mod }N)
    $$
    that is \( y\neq \pm 1(\text{ mod }N)\), then \( \text{gcd}(y-1, N)\) or \( \text{gcd}(y+1, N)\) is a non-trivial factor of \( N\) and can be computed in \(O(L^3)\) operations.

In conclusion, if we can achieve the value of \( r\) in polynomial time, we can factor \( N\) in polynomial time with high probability. This is the Shor‚Äôs algorithm, or the quantum factoring.
