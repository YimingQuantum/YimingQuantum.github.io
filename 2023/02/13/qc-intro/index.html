1. Introduction

Although you don‚Äôt need to have any knowledge of quantum computing to read this article because it‚Äôs the ABC, I recommend you to read at least the first chapter of the book, Quantum Computing and Quantum Information, written by Michael Nielsen and Isaac Chuang. Besides, there is a good review for those who have even no idea of what quantum mechanics is to achieve a general understanding of quantum computing and its trend of development.

This article is about quantum algorithms, and typically we introduce an algorithm first, then demonstrate it on a fake quantum computer. It‚Äôs fake because it‚Äôs realized on classical devices. To be specific, we simulate an environment of a (circuit-based) quantum computer on a classical device like your laptop, and use it to perform quantum algorithms. This way to realize quantum algorithms we sometimes call quantum programming, even though we are not programming on a real quantum computer.

The good news is you don‚Äôt have to start from scratch to ‚Äòbuild‚Äô a fake quantum computer by yourself. There has been some mature and powerful framework to do that, and here are two I recommend:

  • QuEST: a C/C++ library, initially released in 2019 by a development team in the University of Oxford.
  • Qiskit: a Python package, initially released in 2017 by IBM.

Both of them are open-source and can be used on Linux, MacOS or Windows.

I have also tried some other frameworks such as CuQuantum (CUDA C/C++ based, NVIDIA), Q# (Microsoft), and Cirq (Python based, Google). Maybe I will write an article to compare these frameworks in the future. However, if you are familiar with one of them, it‚Äôs easy for you to turn to another.

For each algorithm I introduce, I will put the corresponding codes written with QuEST and Qiskit respectively, so you can choose any framework of the two as you like. To download QuEST or Qisikit, please follow the official instructions.

Attention that this article assumes you are familiar with some concepts in classical computations, including:

  • Bit and the binary representation of numbers.
  • Logic circuits and logic gates;
  • Time complexity.

2. Basics for quantum computing

2.1) Qubit

Let‚Äôs start with a single classical bit. A classical bit can be either \( 0 \) or \( 1 \). We use a new notation for them, that is \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\), and map them to two normalized 2D vectors:

$$
|0\rangle \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\quad
|1\rangle \to \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

This mapping makes sense because a classical bit cannot simultaneously be \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\), thus mathematically, we let the representations of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) be orthogonal.

Under this representation, the NOT gate \( U_N \) can be easily constructed by a \( 2 \times 2\) matrix, i.e.

$$
U_N = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

It‚Äôs easy to check that

$$
U_N |0\rangle = |1\rangle,\quad U_N |1\rangle = |0\rangle
$$

So far we‚Äôve just used a special representation for the classical bit and there‚Äôs nothing quantum.

Now let‚Äôs look at more about the 2D vector. Under the representation above, \(|0\rangle \) and \(|1\rangle \) can exactly compose a set of orthonormal (i.e. orthogonal and normalized) basis of the 2D space, then what about other normalized vectors? Can we view them as some kind of bit as well?

The answer is yes and that kind of bit is called a quantum bit, or qubit. For example,

$$
|\psi\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 1/ \sqrt{2} \\ 1/ \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} =
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} |0\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} |1\rangle
$$

In quantum mechanics, a particle can be a superposition of two or more different states. Here \( |\psi\rangle \) is a superposition of \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \), and this is why \( |\psi\rangle \) is quantum. You may notice that we require the vector representation of a qubit be normalized. This requirement also comes from quantum mechanics, which we will talk in the next section.
