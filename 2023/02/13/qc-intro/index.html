2. Basics for quantum computing

2.1) Qubit

Letâ€™s start with a single classical bit. A classical bit can be either \( 0 \) or \( 1 \). We use a new notation for them, that is \(|0\rangle\) or \(|1\rangle\), and map them to two normalized 2D vectors:

$$
|0\rangle \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\quad
|1\rangle \to \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

This mapping makes sense because a classical bit cannot simultaneously be \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\), thus mathematically, we let the representations of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\) be orthogonal.

Under this representation, the NOT gate \( U_N \) can be easily constructed by a \( 2 \times 2\) matrix, i.e.

$$
U_N = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Itâ€™s easy to check that

$$
U_N |0\rangle = |1\rangle,\quad U_N |1\rangle = |0\rangle
$$

So far weâ€™ve just used a special representation for the classical bit and thereâ€™s nothing quantum.

Now letâ€™s look at more about the 2D vector. Under the representation above, \(|0\rangle \) and \(|1\rangle \) can exactly compose a set of orthonormal (i.e. orthogonal and normalized) basis of the 2D space, then what about other normalized vectors? Can we view them as some kind of bit as well?

The answer is yes and that kind of bit is called a quantum bit, or qubit. For example,

$$
|\psi\rangle = \begin{bmatrix} 1/ \sqrt{2} \\ 1/ \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} =
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} |0\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} |1\rangle
$$

In quantum mechanics, a particle can be a superposition of two or more different states. Here \( |\psi\rangle \) is a superposition of \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \), and this is why \( |\psi\rangle \) is quantum. You may notice that we require the vector representation of a qubit be normalized. This requirement also comes from quantum mechanics, which we will talk in the next section.
